# Generative AI in Software Development: Survey Insights for Curriculum Design at Universities of Applied Sciences

**A Report Based on Industry Survey Data — Rotterdam Area, The Netherlands, 2025**

---

## 1. Introduction

The relationship between higher professional education and the ICT industry has always required active maintenance. Curricula that reflect yesterday's practice produce graduates who arrive underprepared for today's workplace, and the pace of technological change in software development has historically made this alignment difficult to sustain. The emergence of generative AI (GenAI) as a mainstream tool in professional software development has accelerated this challenge to a degree that may be without precedent in the recent history of the field.

Within the span of two to three years, tools such as GitHub Copilot, ChatGPT, and related systems have moved from experimental curiosities to daily professional infrastructure. Developers use them to generate code, debug errors, write documentation, produce test cases, and brainstorm solutions. Organisations that once debated whether to permit these tools now actively encourage their use. The question facing the profession is no longer whether GenAI will be part of software development practice, but how deeply it will penetrate, which human competencies it will supplement, which it will replace, and which it will make more important than ever.

For Universities of Applied Sciences (Hogescholen) in the Netherlands, this shift presents both a responsibility and an opportunity. Hogescholen have historically played a central role in producing practice-oriented software developers for the Dutch ICT industry — graduates who enter the workforce with applicable skills and a readiness for professional collaboration. The HBO pathway is, by design, industry-facing: it promises graduates who can contribute on day one. Fulfilling that promise in a GenAI-transformed industry requires understanding what the industry actually needs from the next generation of developers — not what it needed five years ago, and not what commentators predict it will need in ten.

To develop that understanding, a survey was designed and distributed among ICT professionals working at companies in and around Rotterdam. The survey sought to capture a grounded, practitioner-level picture of how GenAI tools are currently being adopted, how they are experienced in daily professional practice, what problems they introduce, and what skills practitioners believe will define competent software development in the years ahead. The findings of that survey form the empirical basis of this report.

This report draws on the survey results to derive concrete, evidence-based lessons for HBO curriculum design in software engineering and related ICT programmes. Its intended audience is curriculum designers, educational policymakers, and teaching staff at Dutch Universities of Applied Sciences who bear responsibility for preparing the next generation of software developers for a profession that is changing faster than any prior curriculum revision cycle anticipated.

## 2. Methodology

### Survey Design and Distribution

The survey was distributed among ICT professionals employed at companies in the Rotterdam metropolitan area, reaching across more than 100 organisations spanning a range of sizes and sectors. Data collection took place during the summer and autumn of 2025, with responses gathered through an offline questionnaire format — a deliberate methodological choice that ensured responses were considered and self-contained rather than influenced by real-time digital distraction. The survey was anonymous, with no personally identifying information collected.

A total of 31 completed responses were returned and analysed. While this number is modest in absolute terms, the respondent profile — predominantly senior practitioners with direct professional exposure to GenAI tools in organisational settings — ensures that the data reflects informed, experience-based professional judgment rather than general public opinion. The findings should be interpreted as a practitioner consensus from a specific regional ecosystem rather than a nationally or internationally representative sample, and conclusions should be drawn accordingly.

### Survey Structure

The survey comprised 23 questions covering three broad domains. The first domain (Q01–Q06) collected background information about respondents: their professional roles, years of experience, organisation size, programming languages used, and educational background within the Dutch system. The second domain (Q07–Q10) addressed current GenAI adoption: organisational stance toward AI tools, frequency of individual use, the specific tasks for which GenAI is employed, and ratings of how useful it is found for each task. The third domain (Q11–Q23) addressed the future of AI-assisted software development: problems encountered with AI-generated code, anticipated SDLC phase changes, expected skill importance shifts, perceived future challenges, trust in AI output, views on role replacement, the learning curve for junior developers, and qualitative reflections on risk, hiring, and broader professional observations.

Question formats included single-choice categorical, multiple-choice with and without selection caps, Likert-scale rating matrices, and open-ended free text. Q15 contained no responses and was excluded from analysis.

### Analytical Approach

Quantitative questions were analysed using frequency counts, proportional breakdowns, Wilson 95% confidence intervals for robustness at small sample sizes, Shannon entropy as a measure of response consensus, chi-squared goodness-of-fit tests against uniform distributions, and — for scaling questions — ordinal mean scores, standard deviations, medians, and non-parametric bootstrap confidence intervals on the mean. Open-ended responses were analysed through inductive thematic coding, with themes identified from the response content and read against the quantitative findings for convergent validation.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Background of Respondents

The practitioners who participated in this survey represent a profile that is both credible and directly relevant to the curriculum design question. Their collective experience is substantial: nearly half (45%) have been working in software development for more than ten years, and a further 13% for between seven and ten years. Fewer than one in five has three years of experience or less. This is, in other words, a sample of people who have built their professional competency before GenAI tools were available, and who are now integrating those tools into a practice already grounded in traditional software engineering. Their views on what skills matter carry the weight of that experiential foundation.

The roles represented span the professional landscape. Team Leaders and Managers account for just over half the sample, as do Software Engineers — with many respondents occupying both roles simultaneously, reflecting the dual practitioner-manager character common in Dutch ICT firms. Software Architects represent roughly a quarter of respondents. This senior and leadership-heavy profile means the survey is capturing the perspectives of people who not only use GenAI tools themselves but who hire developers, assess junior competency, make tool adoption decisions, and shape the technical direction of their organisations. When they express concern about junior developer learning or advocate for certain skills, they do so from a position of direct managerial and pedagogical responsibility.

Organisationally, the sample is evenly split between small companies of 11 to 50 employees and large enterprises of more than 100, with medium-sized businesses underrepresented. This bimodal distribution reflects something real about the Rotterdam ICT ecosystem — a mix of established large firms and smaller specialised software houses — and it means the survey captures the perspectives of both intensively structured corporate environments and leaner, more agile small-team contexts. These two organisational types often have very different relationships with technology adoption, risk management, and skills investment, and their near-equal representation enriches the breadth of professional outlook in the data.

In terms of educational background, the respondent group reflects the Dutch higher education binary accurately: a slight majority (61%) completed HBO programmes at a University of Applied Sciences, while 39% attended research universities. At the qualification level, bachelor's degrees dominate at 68%, with master's holders at 16% and — notably — doctoral degree holders at 13%, a higher proportion than might be expected in a commercial software development sample. The programming languages in active use skew strongly toward the Microsoft ecosystem: C# leads at 58%, followed by TypeScript at 45%, with Python and JavaScript both at 35%. This language profile signals that much of the daily GenAI experience in this sample is mediated through tools tightly integrated with the Microsoft development environment, particularly GitHub Copilot.

Together, this background portrait describes a workforce of experienced, well-educated practitioners operating in a regionally concentrated but organisationally diverse ICT market, using modern technology stacks, and holding positions of professional authority. Their views on the future of software development skill requirements deserve to be taken seriously by educational institutions whose graduates will enter precisely this workforce.

### 3.2 Usage of Generative AI in Software Development

Perhaps the most consequential single finding in the entire survey is straightforward: every respondent works in an organisation that either permits or actively encourages the use of GenAI tools. There are no exceptions. The debate about whether to allow AI assistance in the workplace has, at least within this sample, been conclusively settled. What the survey reveals is the downstream reality of that settled question — what universal adoption actually looks like when it moves from policy to daily practice.

That daily practice is exactly what the usage data describes. Eighty-four percent of respondents use GenAI tools daily or almost daily. Only five of 31 respondents use these tools less than daily, and none reported avoiding them altogether. GenAI tools are, for this population, not a productivity experiment or an occasional resource — they are professional infrastructure, as routinely present in the development workflow as a code editor or a version control system. The curriculum implication is immediate: graduates entering this workforce will be expected to use these tools fluently from day one, not as an optional enhancement but as a basic professional competency.

The breadth of GenAI application in daily work is equally striking. Respondents use these tools across an average of nearly five distinct task categories simultaneously. The most universal application is brainstorming — used by 81% of respondents — which immediately reframes the popular narrative that GenAI adoption in software development is primarily about code generation productivity. The most widespread use is cognitive and exploratory: developers are using these tools to think through problems, explore architectural options, and decompose requirements before a line of code is written. Code generation and auto-completion follow closely at 74%, and debugging and error explanation at 71%, cementing the core productivity use cases. Further down the distribution, test case generation (48%), code documentation (45%), self-directed learning (45%), and refactoring (42%) all reflect a breadth of integration that extends well beyond the initial code-writing loop.

When respondents are asked to rate how useful they find GenAI for each of these tasks, a revealing hierarchy emerges. Code generation receives the strongest and most unanimous positive rating — no respondent found it not useful, and three quarters rated it Very Useful or Essential. Test case generation, among those who use it, achieves the highest positive rate of all items at 78%, despite being used by fewer than half the sample — a gap suggesting that developers who have not yet integrated AI into their testing workflow are missing a high-value application. Brainstorming and code translation receive strong ratings, and debugging, documentation, and self-directed learning all register as clearly positive.

The picture darkens as the tasks become more contextually demanding and architecturally complex. Security analysis receives the lowest mean utility rating in the entire question — and this is not because respondents have not tried using GenAI for security work, but because they have tried and found it wanting. CI/CD automation and maintenance automation also score in the low range, with no respondents rating either as Essential. These are the domains where deep contextual knowledge of specific infrastructure, organisational standards, and system history is required — precisely the contextual specificity that current AI tools lack. The utility hierarchy across twelve task types is not random: it maps cleanly onto the known capability profile of large language models, rating them highest for structured, pattern-rich, context-independent tasks and lowest for context-sensitive, architecturally integrated, and security-critical work.

This utility profile has direct educational implications. The tasks where GenAI is most useful are those that traditional curricula spend most time teaching: writing code, generating documentation, producing tests. The tasks where GenAI is least useful — security, architecture, system maintenance, code review — are often those that curricula cover later, briefly, or inadequately, because they require the kind of integrated professional judgment that is harder to teach in a classroom setting. The industry data suggests this priority ordering needs to be reconsidered.

### 3.3 The Future of AI in Software Development

#### What Goes Wrong with AI-Generated Code

Understanding where AI tools fail is as important as understanding where they succeed, and the survey's most practically urgent findings concern the problems practitioners actually encounter. The rates are high and concerning. More than 83% of respondents have experienced AI-generated code that did not follow the specification they provided. More than 80% have encountered logic or algorithmic errors in AI output. More than half have experienced security vulnerabilities in AI-generated code, and nearly half have found AI code difficult to debug. These are not occasional edge cases — they are routine professional experiences for the majority of daily GenAI users in the sample.

Security vulnerabilities deserve particular emphasis. Fifty-five percent of respondents report experiencing them, yet security analysis is rated as GenAI's lowest-utility application. The tools introduce security risks that they cannot reliably detect or prevent. This asymmetry — high risk introduction, low risk mitigation — defines the most dangerous capability gap in the current GenAI tooling landscape and directly motivates the skill priority findings that follow.

#### Anticipated Changes to Development Roles and the SDLC

Looking across the horizon, practitioners hold a nuanced and informed view of where AI will and will not change the structure of software development work. The phases they most expect AI to take over within three to five years are documentation (57%) and code writing (50%), followed by debugging and testing at roughly one third each. Requirements analysis, project management, and user experience design are anticipated displacement sites by fewer than one in five respondents — precisely the phases that involve ambiguous human communication, stakeholder negotiation, and contextual judgment.

At the role level, the consensus is clear but not alarmist. Nearly 90% of respondents believe software development will either remain human-centred with change (52%) or will create new roles and shift existing ones (38%), rather than result in meaningful replacement of developers. Only a single respondent — 3.5% of the sample — anticipates significant reduction in developer need. The minority position in this question is not fear of replacement; it is the slightly more conservative view that roles will change but not fundamentally disappear.

Predictions about how much of developer work will involve AI assistance within five years reveal genuine and well-founded uncertainty. The distribution is nearly uniform across the 41–60%, 61–80%, and above-80% bands, with no statistical preference for any single scenario. What is unanimous is the floor: no respondent believes AI involvement will remain below 21%. Whatever the precise trajectory, the direction is unambiguous — upward — and the experienced practitioners in this sample cannot distinguish between moderate and dramatic scenarios because the capability trajectory of the tools genuinely remains unpredictable.

#### The Skill Landscape: What Must Increase and What Will Decline

The most detailed and analytically rich data in the survey concerns expected changes to the importance of specific developer skills. Thirteen skills were rated across a four-level scale from Decreasing to Strongly Increasing in importance, and the results produce a clear and coherent picture that should inform curriculum design directly.

Three skills are identified with near-consensus as strongly increasing in importance. Collaborating with AI as a pair programmer receives the highest positive rate of any item in the question — 97% of respondents see this as increasingly important, with not a single respondent identifying it as declining. Customising and fine-tuning AI tools for development tasks matches this rate exactly. Conducting security audits on AI-generated code achieves the highest mean importance score of all thirteen items, with 76% of respondents selecting Strongly Increasing and only two respondents combined suggesting it will not increase. These three items define the operational core of the future developer role as seen by practitioners: working with AI effectively, directing it purposefully, and catching the security failures it introduces.

A second cluster of skills receives strong majority endorsement as increasing. Debugging and troubleshooting AI-generated code, and reviewing and interpreting AI-generated code logic, both receive 90% positive responses with zero respondents identifying them as declining. Understanding the Foundations of AI scores 86% positive. Fundamental programming concepts — the theoretical and structural bedrock of software engineering — are rated as strongly increasing in importance by 82% of respondents. This last finding is among the most important in the survey for educational institutions: the practitioners who use AI daily, in organisations that encourage its use, are saying with near-consensus that foundational conceptual knowledge becomes more important in an AI-assisted environment, not less.

At the other end of the scale, three skills are identified as declining in importance, and the signals are statistically robust. Memorising syntax and APIs — the ability to recall language-specific implementation details from memory — is rated as Decreasing by 69% of respondents, the clearest single item in the question. Manually writing comments and documentation is rated as Decreasing by 66%. Writing unit tests manually is rated as Decreasing by 41%, though with more disagreement than the other two items. These three form a coherent cluster: they are the skills most directly and reliably substitutable by current AI tools, and the practitioners who use those tools daily have arrived at a calibrated, experience-based judgment that they will matter less.

One item occupies genuinely contested ground: understanding programming language structures. Forty-one percent of respondents see its importance as unchanged, while roughly equal minorities see it as increasing or decreasing. This is the most uncertain and genuinely debated point in the skill landscape — a reflection of a real professional disagreement about whether language-specific structural knowledge remains foundational or becomes redundant as AI handles implementation.

#### What the Profession Considers Most Essential

When asked to nominate, within a three-choice constraint, the skills they consider most essential for future developers, respondents converge with striking clarity. Critical thinking and code review skills is selected by 79% — nearly four in five respondents, using one of their three allocations for a single item. Understanding the limitations of AI-generated code is selected by 59%. Effective prompt engineering comes third at 35%. Secure coding and ethical AI awareness, system design and architecture, and AI/ML fundamentals follow in the 17–28% range.

The ordering of the top two findings carries a message that educational institutions should register carefully. The most essential future skill is not prompt engineering — the distinctively new competency of the GenAI era — but critical thinking and code review, competencies that predate AI by decades and that AI makes more rather than less necessary. Prompt engineering, while valued, ranks third. The profession is saying: knowing how to generate with AI is less important than knowing how to evaluate what AI generates. Generation is the easy part; judgment is the hard part and the enduring part.

#### Challenges, Trust, and the Junior Developer Problem

The challenges practitioners anticipate for the near future are broad and systemic. Security and data privacy risks lead at 72%, followed immediately by developer de-skilling or over-reliance on AI at 69%, and accumulation of technical debt from low-quality AI-generated code at 62%. More than half identify hallucinations and factual inaccuracies in AI output as a major challenge. These are not abstract future risks — they are documented, current realities that respondents expect to intensify.

Trust data reinforces the challenge picture. Fifty-five percent of respondents express zero trust in AI-generated code when it has not been reviewed by a human, and 38% express only partial trust. Only two respondents — 7% of the sample — would mostly trust unreviewed AI output. In a workforce where every single member uses AI tools and every employing organisation supports that use, the universal requirement for human review is the professional norm that has emerged from experience. It is not a regulatory imposition or an academic recommendation — it is a bottom-up consensus formed through daily encounters with the failure modes documented in Q11.

The concern that crystallises most sharply when respondents reflect on junior developers deserves to be treated as the most urgent finding for educational institutions. Seventy-two percent of respondents — the same proportion who do not trust unreviewed AI output, by design coincidence — believe that AI-assisted development reduces the opportunities available to junior developers to learn core problem-solving skills. This figure achieves the highest chi-squared value of any question in the survey, reflecting near-maximal statistical consensus. It is expressed not by academics observing from outside but by team leaders, managers, and senior engineers who hire and supervise junior developers in AI-assisted environments right now.

The mechanism they describe is precise: when a junior developer reaches for an AI tool at the first sign of difficulty, the productive cognitive struggle through which programming competency is built does not occur. The solution arrives, the task is completed, but the understanding is not encoded. Repeated across hundreds of daily interactions over the first years of a career, this pattern produces practitioners who can direct AI fluently but cannot reason independently about the problems the tools are solving. Several respondents extend this concern to its practical endpoint: problems that AI cannot solve will also be unsolvable to developers whose problem-solving skills were never developed. One respondent raises the further concern about systems built primarily through prompting that eventually reach a complexity threshold where neither further prompting nor manual engineering can maintain them — a uniquely dangerous failure mode with no historical precedent.

The open-ended responses in Q21, Q22, and Q23 add texture and specificity to these patterns. On risk, the dominant theme across the 25 responses is what one practitioner calls the illusion of correctness — code that appears syntactically clean and plausible but is semantically wrong, insecure, or unmaintainable. Respondents note that AI defaults to the most common solution in its training data rather than the best solution for the specific context, systematically omits non-functional requirements like security and scalability unless explicitly prompted, and produces code that is difficult to maintain precisely because it was not thought through by the person who will later need to modify it.

On hiring, a clear emerging philosophy runs through the Q22 responses: assess judgment, not production. Multiple respondents, independently of one another, describe a shift from testing whether a candidate can write code to testing whether they can evaluate, critique, and improve code — including code that an AI generated. One practitioner summarises the direction simply: "developers will be expected to act as intelligent stewards of AI tools, not just coders." Another notes that traditional syntax-and-algorithm interview tasks are becoming obsolete, replaced by assessments of analytical skill, architectural thinking, and the ability to identify failures in AI-generated output quickly and precisely.

Among the additional comments in Q23, one stands out for its immediate practical urgency. A respondent reports that their organisation has already discontinued its MBO internship programme because the foundational knowledge level of incoming interns — degraded, in their assessment, by AI-assisted education that bypassed the development of basic competency — is insufficient for supervised professional work. HBO graduates, the same respondent notes, are approaching the same threshold. This is not a projected risk; it is a current operational reality in at least one Rotterdam-area ICT company, and it signals that the educational pipeline consequences of AI-assisted learning may already be visible at the point of entry to the profession.

## 4. Discussion: Lessons for HBO Curriculum Design in Software Engineering

The survey data, read as a whole, delivers a coherent and actionable set of lessons for Universities of Applied Sciences in the Netherlands. These lessons are not speculative extrapolations from technology trends — they are derived from the stated professional experience and considered judgment of the practitioners who will hire the next generation of HBO graduates and supervise their early development. Each lesson is grounded in specific survey findings and carries direct implications for curriculum content, pedagogical approach, and assessment design.

### 4.1 Foundational Knowledge Is Not Becoming Less Important — It Is Becoming More Important

The single most important corrective message in this survey for any educational institution tempted to simplify or shorten its foundational curriculum is this: 82% of experienced daily GenAI users rate Fundamental Concepts as strongly or slightly increasing in importance for future developers. This is not a nostalgic defence of the status quo — it is a forward-looking professional assessment from people who understand both what GenAI can do and what it cannot.

The logic is coherent and should inform curriculum policy directly. When code generation is partially delegated to AI, the developer's primary value shifts from producing code to evaluating it. Evaluating AI-generated code — determining whether it is correct, efficient, maintainable, secure, and fit for the specific context — requires precisely the foundational understanding of data structures, algorithms, computational complexity, and software design principles that implementation-focused teaching builds. A developer who cannot reason about algorithmic correctness cannot reliably judge whether AI-generated code is algorithmically sound, even if they could not have written it themselves without assistance.

Curriculum designers should resist the argument that foundational content can be reduced because AI handles implementation. The data suggests the reverse: implementation is increasingly AI-handled, which makes the conceptual layer above implementation more important, not less, because it is the layer that cannot be delegated. Mathematics, algorithms, data structures, design patterns, and software engineering principles are not becoming obsolete — they are becoming the distinguishing competency of developers who can work with AI effectively versus those who can only work with it.

### 4.2 Code Review and Critical Evaluation Must Become Core Curriculum, Not Peripheral Skills

Critical thinking and code review skills is selected as the most essential future competency by 79% of respondents — the clearest consensus finding in the entire survey. Understanding the limitations of AI-generated code is selected by 59%. Together, these two competencies define what might be called the AI-oversight core: the capacity to evaluate what AI produces, know where it is likely to fail, and catch those failures before they reach production.

These competencies are not currently treated as core curriculum in most software engineering programmes. Code review tends to appear in later-stage project work, if at all, and is rarely assessed as a standalone skill. Critical evaluation of code correctness, security, and architectural soundness is often implied rather than explicitly taught. The survey data suggests this must change. If practitioners identify these as the most essential skills for the profession's future, and if they simultaneously report that 83% of AI-generated code does not follow specifications and 80% contains logic errors, then teaching graduates to identify these failures systematically is not optional enrichment — it is professional preparation.

Practically, this implies introducing structured code review activities early in programmes and treating them as explicitly assessed competencies. Students should learn to read and evaluate code — including code produced by AI tools — with the same rigour that traditional programmes apply to writing code. Review checklists, evaluation rubrics for AI-generated output, and practice in identifying logic errors, security vulnerabilities, and specification mismatches should become standard components of the curriculum, not extras reserved for final-year projects.

### 4.3 Security Competency Requires Significant Curriculum Investment

The security finding in this survey is four-layered and each layer points to the same educational conclusion. GenAI tools are rated as least useful for security analysis of all twelve task categories. Security vulnerabilities in AI-generated code are reported by 55% of respondents. Security and data privacy risks are identified as the top anticipated future challenge by 72%. Conducting security audits on AI-generated code achieves the highest mean importance score of all thirteen skill items in Q14. Four independently framed questions converge on the same finding: security is the domain where AI creates the most risk and provides the least assistance, and where human competency must compensate for the gap.

Current HBO software engineering curricula typically include security as a module — an important but bounded component of a broader programme. The survey data argues for reconsidering this proportionality. If security audit of AI-generated code is the most important single skill for the future developer role, as the Q14 data establishes, then security cannot remain a bounded module. It needs to be integrated throughout the curriculum as a lens through which all development activities are viewed: when students write code, they should be learning to think about its security implications; when they review code, security audit should be an explicit evaluation criterion; when they work with AI-generated output, identifying security vulnerabilities should be a practised and assessed skill.

Specifically, graduates should leave HBO programmes able to recognise common vulnerability patterns in AI-generated code — injection vulnerabilities, authentication weaknesses, insecure data handling — and understand why AI tools tend to underweight these concerns unless explicitly prompted. The practical security gap that practitioners document is not closing by itself; education must close it.

### 4.4 AI Tool Proficiency Is a Professional Requirement, But Not the Primary Curriculum Goal

The survey establishes clearly that graduates entering this workforce will need to use AI tools competently and routinely. Collaborating with AI as a pair programmer and customising AI tools for development tasks are each rated as strongly increasing in importance by 97% of respondents. Effective prompt engineering is selected as an essential future skill by 35%. Graduates who arrive unable to work with these tools fluently will be at a disadvantage from day one.

However, the data also establishes clearly that AI tool proficiency ranks third in priority, behind critical thinking and code review (79%) and understanding AI limitations (59%). Programmes that orient themselves primarily around teaching AI tool usage — how to prompt, how to configure, how to integrate Copilot into a workflow — are optimising for the tertiary priority while potentially neglecting the primary ones. The professional consensus is not "teach them to use AI"; it is "teach them to evaluate what AI produces, and give them the foundational knowledge to do that well."

The curriculum implication is one of framing rather than content replacement. AI tools should be taught and used throughout HBO programmes — as productivity tools, as learning aids, and as objects of critical study. But their use should be embedded within a pedagogical frame that consistently positions the student as the evaluator and judge of AI output, not the passive recipient of it. When a student uses Copilot to generate a function, the pedagogical moment is not the generation — it is the review: is this correct? Is it secure? Is it appropriate for this context? Does it follow the specification? Can I explain why it works?

### 4.5 The Pedagogical Challenge: Preserving Productive Struggle in AI-Enabled Learning

The survey's sharpest and most urgent message for educational institutions concerns the learning process itself. Seventy-two percent of practitioners believe AI-assisted development reduces the opportunities available to junior developers to learn core problem-solving skills. The mechanism is well understood: when AI provides solutions on demand, the productive cognitive struggle through which deep understanding is built is bypassed. Competency appears to develop — students complete assignments, projects work, code runs — while the underlying conceptual foundation is not being built.

This is an acute pedagogical challenge precisely because it is invisible. A student using AI assistance to complete a programming assignment will produce output that looks competent. Their grade may be unaffected. Their portfolio may look strong. But the problem-solving process that would have encoded the underlying concepts did not occur, and this absence only becomes apparent when AI is unavailable, when problems fall outside AI's training distribution, or when the student is required to maintain, debug, and extend code that was generated without full understanding.

Several responses in the open-ended section add a structural dimension to this concern. One practitioner reports that their organisation has stopped accepting MBO interns because the foundational knowledge level — degraded by AI-assisted education — is insufficient for professional work, and flags that HBO graduates are approaching the same threshold. Another notes that the entry-level tasks that traditionally provided safe learning opportunities for junior developers — boilerplate code, well-defined features, simple bugs — are precisely the tasks AI has automated, narrowing the professional on-ramp precisely when its difficulty increases.

For curriculum designers and teaching staff, the practical response is not to prohibit AI tools in educational settings — that would be both impractical and educationally counterproductive. It is to design learning activities that preserve the productive struggle that AI can bypass. This means:

- Assessments that evaluate reasoning processes and documented decision-making, not only working code as a final product.
- AI-free assessment components where students demonstrate that foundational competency is genuinely present, not masked by tool assistance.
- Debugging and explanation exercises where students must account for code behaviour without generating new solutions — activating comprehension and diagnostic reasoning independently of generation.
- Explicit metacognitive guidance about the difference between using AI to accelerate work students already understand and using AI to bypass work they do not yet understand.

### 4.6 The Hiring Signal: What Employers Will Assess

The Q22 responses describe an emerging shift in how ICT employers will assess candidates that has direct implications for what HBO programmes should prepare graduates for. The direction is clear across multiple independent responses: hiring will move toward assessing judgment, foundational knowledge, and evaluative capacity, and away from assessing syntax recall and code production under artificial constraint. "Developers will be expected to act as intelligent stewards of AI tools, not just coders." "The requirements will change from knowing coding syntax to knowing fundamentals and the ability to review AI-generated code." "No technical but rather critical thinking and challenging fundamental knowledge."

This does not mean technical assessment disappears — it means technical assessment changes character. The ability to write a sorting algorithm from scratch under whiteboard conditions is becoming less relevant than the ability to read an AI-generated implementation, identify its weaknesses, and explain why an alternative approach would be more appropriate for a specific context. HBO graduates who are strong on the latter and weaker on the former will be better positioned in the emerging hiring landscape than the reverse.

Programmes should consider whether their current assessment methods are building toward the skills employers will assess. If final-year projects, internship placements, and portfolio submissions are evaluated primarily on working code quantity, they may be rewarding the same surface productivity that AI tools provide rather than the evaluative and architectural depth that employers are beginning to value more highly.

### 4.7 The Human Layer Is Not Optional

Across every dimension of the survey — trust, risk, skills, challenges, hiring, and professional norms — one principle recurs with consistency: human oversight of AI-generated output is not a transitional phase to be superseded as AI capabilities improve, but a permanent professional responsibility. Fifty-five percent of daily AI users do not trust AI-generated code without human review. The skills rated as most essential are those that enable effective human oversight. The challenges rated as most serious are those that result from inadequate human oversight. The risks described in open-ended responses are almost universally risks of insufficient human judgment applied to AI output.

For educational institutions, this principle translates into a professional identity message that should run throughout HBO software engineering programmes: the developer's value in an AI-assisted environment lies in judgment, not production. The ability to evaluate what AI generates, to understand where it fails and why, to maintain accountability for code that enters production, and to build systems that are not only functional but secure, maintainable, and trustworthy — these are the competencies that define professional value when production itself is partially automated.

This is ultimately an educational framing question as much as a content question. Programmes that frame software engineering as the activity of building working software need to broaden that frame to encompass the activity of taking responsibility for software quality in an environment where much of the construction is AI-assisted. The developer who understands what they are accountable for, and has the competency to meet that accountability, is the developer the industry needs and is actively looking for.

## 5. Conclusion

This survey captures a moment of significant professional transition in the Dutch ICT industry. GenAI tools have moved from novelty to infrastructure, from permitted to encouraged, from occasionally used to daily habit. The practitioners who completed this survey are navigating that transition in real time, and their reflections on what it requires — in skills, in judgment, in professional culture — constitute one of the most direct and evidence-based signals available to educational institutions about what the next generation of developers needs.

The core message for Universities of Applied Sciences is not that everything must change. It is that the things that need to change are specific, identifiable, and urgent. Foundational knowledge must be protected and reinforced, not sacrificed to make room for tool tutorials. Security competency must be elevated from module to curriculum thread. Code review and critical evaluation must become core, assessed, foundational skills. AI tool proficiency must be taught within a frame of critical judgment rather than passive adoption. And the learning process itself must be designed to preserve the productive struggle that builds genuine competency, even as AI tools make it easier than ever to bypass that struggle.

The graduates who will thrive in this industry — according to the practitioners who will hire and supervise them — are those who can evaluate what AI generates, understand where it fails, take responsibility for what goes into production, and direct AI tools with the architectural and conceptual judgment that only deep foundational knowledge enables. That is what HBO programmes must build.
